Ch 10. Two-layer QG & available potential energy

Overview

A two-layer fluid decomposes uniquely into a barotropic mode (the depth mean, obeying exactly Ch. 7/18’s ordinary barotropic vorticity equation) and a baroclinic mode (the inter-layer difference, obeying exactly Ch. 8’s single-layer QG equation, and carrying all the available potential energy). This chapter derives that exact decoupling, then breaks it on purpose: seeding a disturbance purely in the baroclinic mode and watching nonlinear self-advection leak a barotropic component into existence where none was placed.

The model

What to look for

  1. Start pure, watch it leak. The initial condition lives entirely in the baroclinic mode (ψbt=0\psi_{bt}=0 exactly, to machine precision — the mode-energy panel confirms it at t=0t=0). Run, and watch the barotropic energy curve lift off from zero: the linear decoupling is exact, so every bit of barotropic flow you see was manufactured by the nonlinear term J(ψbc,qbc)J(\psi_{bc},q_{bc}), which projects back onto the barotropic equation.
  2. Two vortices, two personalities. Look at the two mode fields side by side as the run evolves: the baroclinic part stays compact (screened at the scale 1/2F1/\sqrt{2F}, Ch. 8’s mechanism with LRL_R replaced by the baroclinic deformation radius), while the growing barotropic part spreads — it has no deformation radius to stop it.
  3. FF is the knob that matters. Raise FF (thinner layers / weaker stratification): the baroclinic mode becomes more tightly screened and the leak into the barotropic mode changes character. Compare the final barotropic-energy fraction at F=0.5F=0.5 and F=3F=3 before reading exercise 3.
  4. Read the two panels as two earlier chapters. The snapshot browser shows qbtq_{bt} and qbcq_{bc} side by side: the left panel is a flow obeying exactly Ch. 7’s barotropic vorticity equation, the right panel one obeying exactly Ch. 8’s single-layer QG equation — and at frame 0 the left panel is identically zero. What couples them afterward is nothing in the linear physics, only advection. That change of glasses — from two similar-looking layers to two familiar, decoupled equations — is the whole point of the chapter.

Both fluids

  • Ocean: newly-formed mesoscale eddies are overwhelmingly baroclinic at birth (generated by Ch. 16’s instability, which draws only on APE); the slow barotropization this notebook’s leak illustrates in miniature is a leading theory for how eddy energy ultimately escapes into the basin-scale, nearly-barotropic circulation.
  • Atmosphere: the storm track’s baroclinic eddies feed the barotropic-dominated jet stream through essentially this same nonlinear route — Ch. 18’s inverse cascade needs a source, and baroclinic instability plus this mode-coupling leak is where its energy actually comes from.
